# Saint Matthias (d. c. 80)

### Saint Matthias (d. c. 80)

**Feast day:** May 14 (formerly February 24 in the older Roman calendar) | **Age at death:** unknown | **Martyrdom:** tradition variously gives Ethiopia, Cappadocia, or Jerusalem; manner uncertain (stoning or crucifixion are both attested)
**Symbol in iconography:** an axe or a halberd; a lance
**Known for:** chosen by lot to replace Judas; the apostle whose entire biblical witness fits in twelve verses

Matthias is named only once in the New Testament, in Acts 1:15–26. Peter, addressing the gathered disciples shortly before Pentecost, declares that the place vacated by Judas must be filled by someone who has been with the apostolic company "during all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us, beginning from the baptism of John until the day when he was taken up from us — one of these must become a witness with us to his resurrection" (Acts 1:21–22). Two are proposed: Joseph called Barsabbas (also known as Justus) and Matthias. After prayer, they cast lots, "and the lot fell on Matthias; and he was added to the eleven apostles" (Acts 1:26). The casting of lots is the last occurrence of this Old Testament practice in the New Testament; after Pentecost, the Spirit will guide the Church directly. Of Matthias's subsequent mission Scripture says nothing. Tradition gives him a ministry in Judea, Ethiopia, or Cappadocia, and a martyr's death.

## Saint Paul, Apostle to the Gentiles (c. AD 5 – c. AD 67)

**Feast day:** June 29 (with Peter); also January 25 (Conversion of Saint Paul) | **Age at death:** approximately 60–67 | **Martyrdom:** beheaded by sword in Rome under Nero, c. AD 67 (a Roman citizen was entitled to execution by sword rather than crucifixion); his tomb is venerated under the Papal Basilica of Saint Paul Outside the Walls
**Symbol in iconography:** a sword (instrument of his martyrdom; also the "sword of the Spirit," Ephesians 6:17) and a book (his Letters)
**Known for:** the Apostle to the Gentiles; conversion on the Damascus road; the thirteen Letters that bear his name (with Hebrews traditionally placed at the end of his corpus though its authorship is debated)

Saul of Tarsus was a Pharisee, a Roman citizen by birth, a tentmaker by trade, and a violent persecutor of the Church. He was present, consenting, at the stoning of Stephen the first martyr (Acts 7:58–8:1). Traveling to Damascus with letters of authority to arrest Christians, he was struck to the ground by a blinding light and heard the voice: "Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?" — "Who are you, Lord?" — "I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting" (Acts 9:3–5). The encounter shattered and remade him. He was baptized by Ananias, withdrew to Arabia for a time, and then began the missionary career that would carry the gospel across Asia Minor, into Macedonia and Greece, and finally to Rome, where he wrote his last Letters under house arrest and ultimately died. Though he was not one of the Twelve, the Church has from the beginning numbered him among the Apostles with full apostolic authority. He himself argued the point: "Am I not an apostle? Have I not seen Jesus our Lord?" (1 Corinthians 9:1). His Letters — to the Romans, Corinthians, Galatians, Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians, Thessalonians, to Timothy, Titus, and Philemon — are the longest and most theologically developed body of writing in the New Testament and the indispensable foundation of Christian theology.

## The Apostolic College

The Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that "the Lord Jesus endowed his community with a structure that will remain until the Kingdom is fully achieved. Before all else there is the choice of the Twelve with Peter as their head. Because they represent the twelve tribes of Israel, they are the foundation stones of the new Jerusalem. The Twelve and the other disciples share in Christ's mission and his power, but also in his lot" (CCC 765). The bishops are "the successors of the apostles by divine institution"; whoever listens to them listens to Christ, and whoever despises them despises Christ and him who sent Christ (CCC 858, citing Luke 10:16).

This is the doctrine of apostolic succession. It is not a sentimental link to the past but the visible structure of the Church's continuity with the One who sent them. The bishop in communion with the bishop of Rome is the contemporary apostle. The whole sacramental life of the Church — Eucharist, ordination, the forgiveness of sins, the anointing of the sick — depends on this unbroken chain.
