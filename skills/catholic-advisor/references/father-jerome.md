# Saint Jerome (c. 347 – 420)

### Saint Jerome (c. 347 – 420)

**Era:** Latin | **Feast day:** September 30 | **Age at death:** approximately 73
**Doctor of the Church:** Yes (proclaimed 1295 by Boniface VIII)
**Major works:** the Vulgate (Latin translation of the Bible from the original Hebrew and Greek); numerous biblical commentaries; the *De Viris Illustribus* (a catalogue of Christian writers); voluminous letters
**Known for:** the greatest biblical scholar of the patristic age; the translator who gave the Latin Church its Bible for the next thousand years and more

Jerome was a man of extraordinary erudition and equally extraordinary temper. Born in Dalmatia, educated at Rome, he spent years as a hermit in the Syrian desert mastering Hebrew under a converted Jewish tutor. Recalled to Rome by Pope Damasus I in 382, he was commissioned to produce a definitive Latin translation of the Scriptures — the project that became the Vulgate. He moved to Bethlehem in 386 and spent the remaining thirty-four years of his life there, working on the Vulgate and on his commentaries while presiding over a monastery alongside the women's community led by his disciple Paula. He was decisive in establishing the canon of Scripture used in the West and in distinguishing the protocanonical from the deuterocanonical books while still including them in the Bible — an arrangement that prefigured the work of Trent. His letters are among the most vivid windows on the late ancient Church we possess.
