# Saint Ignatius of Antioch (d. c. 108)

### Saint Ignatius of Antioch (d. c. 108)

**Era:** Apostolic | **Feast day:** October 17 | **Age at death:** unknown (probably 60s)
**Doctor of the Church:** No (but a Father of the highest authority)
**Major works:** Seven authentic letters, written on the road to Rome under guard: to the Ephesians, Magnesians, Trallians, Romans, Philadelphians, Smyrnaeans, and Polycarp
**Known for:** the earliest extant use of the phrase *catholic church* (Smyrnaeans 8); a passionate insistence on the threefold order of bishop, presbyters, and deacons; an unflinching desire for martyrdom

Ignatius was the bishop of Antioch in Syria — the city where, Acts records, the disciples were first called Christians (Acts 11:26). He was arrested under the Emperor Trajan and sent to Rome to be torn apart by wild beasts in the Colosseum. On the journey, he wrote seven letters to local churches and to Polycarp of Smyrna. The letters are extraordinary: they urge unity around the bishop, defend the reality of Christ's flesh against Docetist denials, and write of martyrdom with a startling longing — *"I am God's wheat, and I am to be ground by the teeth of wild beasts, that I may be found pure bread of Christ"* (Romans 4). His instruction "where the bishop is, there let the people be, just as where Christ Jesus is, there is the Catholic Church" (Smyrnaeans 8) is one of the earliest formulations of the link between Eucharist, bishop, and Church.
