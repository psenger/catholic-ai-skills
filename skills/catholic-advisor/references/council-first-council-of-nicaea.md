# 1. First Council of Nicaea (325)

### 1. First Council of Nicaea (325)

**Convoked by:** Pope Sylvester I (with the active sponsorship of the Emperor Constantine, who summoned the bishops) | **Sessions:** May–July (approximately) | **Bishops present:** approximately 318, traditionally
**Heresy addressed:** Arianism — the teaching of Arius of Alexandria that the Son was created and not co-eternal with the Father
**Key teachings:**
- The Son is *homoousios* — of the same substance — with the Father
- The original Nicene Creed (later expanded at Constantinople I)
- The dating of Easter (the first Sunday after the first full moon after the spring equinox)

Nicaea is the foundational dogmatic council of the Church. Confronting the Arian denial of Christ's full divinity, the bishops — led by Athanasius of Alexandria (then a young deacon, soon to be bishop) — defined that the Son is "begotten not made, of one being with the Father." The Greek word *homoousios* ("of the same substance") was chosen precisely because Arius could not accept it: it ruled out any reduction of the Son to a creature, however exalted. The Creed of Nicaea was the Church's first universally binding statement of faith and the prototype of the Christian creedal tradition. The Council also legislated on Church discipline (the twenty Canons of Nicaea), regulated the dating of Easter, and addressed the Meletian schism in Egypt.
