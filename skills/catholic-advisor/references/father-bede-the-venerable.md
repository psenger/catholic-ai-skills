# Saint Bede the Venerable (c. 672 – 735)

### Saint Bede the Venerable (c. 672 – 735)

**Era:** Latin (counted as last Father in some traditions; certainly the closing voice of the patristic tradition in the Anglo-Saxon world) | **Feast day:** May 25 | **Age at death:** approximately 63
**Doctor of the Church:** Yes (proclaimed 1899 by Leo XIII — *Doctor Anglorum*, Doctor of the English)
**Major works:** *Ecclesiastical History of the English People* (in five books); biblical commentaries; lives of saints; computus (mathematical works on the dating of Easter)
**Known for:** the father of English history; the monk of Jarrow whose pen produced the most influential single book of the Anglo-Saxon era; the man who popularized the *Anno Domini* dating system

Bede was given to the monastery of Wearmouth–Jarrow as a boy of seven and remained there until his death sixty years later. His *Ecclesiastical History of the English People*, completed in 731, is the foundational document of English national identity and the principal source for the conversion of the English. He was the first major historian to date events consistently *from the Incarnation* (*Anno Domini*), the system that gradually became standard across Christendom. He died on the eve of Ascension Day, 735, having dictated the final sentence of his translation of the Gospel of John to his scribe. His designation as the closing Father of the Latin West is more honorary than strict; in any case he is the last theologian widely regarded as patristic in temperament.

## The Patristic Witness

The Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that Sacred Tradition and Sacred Scripture make up a single sacred deposit of the Word of God, entrusted to the Church (CCC 84). The writings of the Fathers are not themselves Scripture, nor are they automatically infallible. But they are the privileged witnesses to how the Church received, lived, and handed on the apostolic faith in the centuries closest to the apostles themselves. Where the Fathers agree unanimously on a point of faith or morals, that consensus is regarded as a witness to the apostolic Tradition (CCC 113, 688).

The Catechism includes the writings of the Holy Fathers among the means by which the Magisterium expresses the faith of the Church (CCC 688). This is why every great Catholic theologian — from Aquinas to Newman to Ratzinger — has read the Fathers as a school. The faith does not begin afresh in every generation; it grows in continuity with its own past, and the Fathers are the closest contemporaries of its source.
