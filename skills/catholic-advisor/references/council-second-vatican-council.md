# 21. Second Vatican Council (1962–1965)

### 21. Second Vatican Council (1962–1965)

**Convoked by:** Pope John XXIII; concluded under Pope Paul VI | **Sessions:** four (autumn of 1962, 1963, 1964, 1965)
**Issue addressed:** the renewal of the Church's life and mission, the engagement of the Church with the modern world, ecumenism, religious liberty, the liturgy, and the relationship of the Catholic Church to other Christian communities, to Judaism, and to other religions
**Sixteen documents:** four Constitutions, nine Decrees, three Declarations

**Constitutions:**
- *Sacrosanctum Concilium* (1963) — Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy, mandating the reform of the rites and permitting the use of the vernacular alongside Latin
- *Lumen Gentium* (1964) — Dogmatic Constitution on the Church, recovering the image of the Church as the People of God and the Mystical Body, and defining the collegiality of the bishops with the pope as their head
- *Dei Verbum* (1965) — Dogmatic Constitution on Divine Revelation, treating Scripture and Tradition as a single sacred deposit with the Magisterium as their authentic interpreter
- *Gaudium et Spes* (1965) — Pastoral Constitution on the Church in the Modern World, addressing the dignity of the human person, marriage and family, culture, economic life, peace, and the relationship of the Church to the human community

**Nine Decrees:** *Inter Mirifica* (means of social communication); *Orientalium Ecclesiarum* (the Eastern Catholic Churches); *Unitatis Redintegratio* (ecumenism); *Christus Dominus* (the pastoral office of bishops); *Perfectae Caritatis* (the renewal of religious life); *Optatam Totius* (priestly training); *Apostolicam Actuositatem* (the apostolate of the laity); *Ad Gentes* (missionary activity); *Presbyterorum Ordinis* (the ministry and life of priests)

**Three Declarations:** *Gravissimum Educationis* (Christian education); *Nostra Aetate* (the Church's relation to non-Christian religions, decisively reframing Catholic theology of Judaism after the Shoah); *Dignitatis Humanae* (religious liberty — defining the right of every human person to immunity from coercion in religious matters, while also affirming the duty to seek the truth)

Vatican II is the council whose interpretation continues to be debated. Two principles, taught by Pope Benedict XVI in his 2005 address to the Roman Curia, govern Catholic reception: *the hermeneutic of reform in continuity* — the council must be read as continuous with the entire tradition of the Church; and *not the hermeneutic of rupture* — the council did not start the Church over. Within that framework, the council's documents continue to shape the Church's liturgy, ecumenical engagement, dialogue with Judaism and world religions, social teaching, and self-understanding.

## Reading the Councils Today

The Catechism teaches that the deposit of faith is transmitted by Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition together, and that the Magisterium of the Church — the bishops in communion with the bishop of Rome — has been entrusted with the authentic interpretation of this deposit (CCC 84–87). Ecumenical councils are the most solemn exercise of that Magisterium, and their teaching, when definitive, is irreformable. The councils are therefore not optional resources for the Catholic; they are the binding voice of the Church teaching in council.

The Catechism also teaches the development of doctrine: the apostolic Tradition develops in the Church under the Spirit's guidance, and "the Christian faithful… grow in understanding of the realities and the words which have been handed down" (CCC 94, citing *Dei Verbum* 8). Later councils do not contradict earlier ones; they unfold them. Nicaea is presupposed by Chalcedon; Chalcedon is presupposed by Constantinople III. Trent is presupposed by Vatican I; Vatican I is presupposed and completed by Vatican II.

This makes the councils a unity. To read them rightly is to read them together — and to read them all in the light of the Christ who is their center.
