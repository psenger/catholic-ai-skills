# 2. First Council of Constantinople (381)

### 2. First Council of Constantinople (381)

**Convoked by:** Pope Damasus I (summoned by the Emperor Theodosius I) | **Bishops present:** 150 Eastern bishops (no Western bishops attended; papal ratification came subsequently)
**Heresy addressed:** Macedonianism (Pneumatomachianism) — the denial of the full divinity of the Holy Spirit
**Key teachings:**
- Expanded the Creed of Nicaea with explicit articles on the Holy Spirit ("the Lord and giver of life… who with the Father and the Son together is worshipped and glorified")
- Affirmed the divinity of the Holy Spirit against the Macedonians
- The bishop of Constantinople received primacy of honor after the bishop of Rome (Canon 3) — a canon disputed in its full implications

Constantinople I completed the trinitarian work begun at Nicaea by addressing the third Person of the Trinity. The doctrinal driver was Macedonius and those who denied that the Spirit was God. The Cappadocian Fathers — Basil of Caesarea (who died in 379, before the council, but whose theology shaped its work), Gregory of Nazianzus (who presided briefly), and Gregory of Nyssa — were the architects of the council's pneumatology. The Creed promulgated at Constantinople, now called the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed, is the creed prayed at every Sunday Mass in the Roman Rite and in nearly every other liturgical tradition. The council also confronted Apollinarianism, the teaching that Christ lacked a human soul.
