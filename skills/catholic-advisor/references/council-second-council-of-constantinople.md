# 5. Second Council of Constantinople (553)

### 5. Second Council of Constantinople (553)

**Convoked by:** Pope Vigilius (under pressure from the Emperor Justinian I)
**Issue addressed:** the so-called "Three Chapters" — writings of Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrus, and Ibas of Edessa with Nestorian tendencies — and a renewed condemnation of Origenism
**Key teachings:**
- Condemned the Three Chapters
- Reaffirmed Chalcedon
- Condemned a number of Origenist propositions

This council was the least edifying of the early seven from a procedural standpoint: Justinian convoked it, dragooned Vigilius into endorsing it, and used it to address Eastern critics of Chalcedon by symbolic condemnations. The council nevertheless produced lasting fruit: the condemnation of certain Origenist excesses (the pre-existence of souls, the *apokatastasis* or universal final restoration of all things including the demons) clarified Catholic eschatology. Origen himself is not anathematized by name in the council's canons, and Catholic tradition has generally preserved him as a great if uncanonized teacher whose errors were corrected by his disciples.
