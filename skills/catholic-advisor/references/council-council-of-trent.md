# 19. Council of Trent (1545–1563)

### 19. Council of Trent (1545–1563)

**Convoked by:** Pope Paul III; continued under Pope Julius III and concluded under Pope Pius IV | **Sessions:** twenty-five, in three principal periods
**Issue addressed:** the comprehensive response of the Catholic Church to the Protestant Reformation
**Key teachings:**
- Canon of Scripture (Session IV, 1546): definitively defined the canon of seventy-two books (forty-five Old Testament including the deuterocanonicals, twenty-seven New Testament) against the Protestant rejection of the deuterocanonicals
- Sacred Tradition (Session IV): defined that the Gospel is contained in both Scripture and unwritten apostolic Tradition, received and held with equal devotion (*pari pietatis affectu*); this is later refined by *Dei Verbum* at Vatican II
- Original sin (Session V, 1546)
- Justification (Session VI, 1547): the great dogmatic decree affirming that justification is by grace, through faith, working in love; that grace is freely given but that human cooperation is real; that good works performed in grace are truly meritorious. The decree was the principal Catholic response to Luther's *sola fide*
- The Seven Sacraments (Session VII and following): defined the seven sacraments of baptism, confirmation, Eucharist, penance, anointing of the sick, holy orders, and matrimony, against Protestant reductions of the number
- The Eucharist (Session XIII, 1551): Real Presence, transubstantiation, the worship of the Eucharist
- Penance and Anointing of the Sick (Session XIV, 1551)
- The Sacrifice of the Mass (Session XXII, 1562): the Mass is a true and proper sacrifice, offered for the living and the dead
- Holy Orders (Session XXIII, 1563)
- Matrimony (Session XXIV, 1563): defined matrimony as a sacrament and instituted the formal requirements for valid Catholic marriage (*Tametsi*)
- Purgatory, the invocation of saints, indulgences (Session XXV, 1563)

Trent is the watershed council of the modern Catholic Church. It produced not only the definitions above but a comprehensive reform of Catholic life: the establishment of seminaries for the formation of clergy, the obligation of bishops to reside in their dioceses, the reform of the Mass (the so-called Tridentine Missal of Pius V, promulgated in 1570), the reform of the Breviary, and the Roman Catechism (1566). Trent shaped the Catholic Church for four centuries — until Vatican II, in many areas, undertook the next great reform.
