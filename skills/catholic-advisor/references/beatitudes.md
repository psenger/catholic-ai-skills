The Beatitudes do not abolish the Ten Commandments; they fulfill them (Matthew 5:17). See also the companion file `ten-commandments.md`.

## The Beatitudes in Catholic Theology

The Beatitudes are the heart of Jesus' preaching. They take up the promises made to the chosen people since Abraham and fulfill them by ordering them no longer merely to the possession of a land but to the Kingdom of Heaven (CCC 1716). They depict the countenance of Jesus Christ and portray his charity; they express the vocation of the faithful associated with the glory of his Passion and Resurrection; they shed light on the actions and attitudes characteristic of the Christian life; they are the paradoxical promises that sustain hope in the midst of tribulations (CCC 1717).

The Beatitudes respond to the natural desire for happiness placed by God in every human heart, in order to draw us to himself (CCC 1718). They reveal the goal of human existence: the ultimate end of human acts is God, who alone can fulfill our desires; this is the beatific vision (CCC 1719–1721).

## The Eight Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3–10)

### 1. Blessed are the poor in spirit (Matthew 5:3)

**Promise:** the kingdom of heaven | **Virtue cluster:** humility, detachment, hope | **Catechism:** CCC 2544–2547

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."

Poverty of spirit names the interior disposition of those who know they have nothing apart from God and depend on him entirely. It is not romanticized destitution and not the contempt of material goods; it is the freedom that comes from refusing to make any creature an idol. The Catechism teaches that Jesus enjoins his disciples to prefer him to everything and everyone, and proposes voluntary humility as a means of attaining detachment from riches (CCC 2544). All Christ's faithful are to direct their affections rightly, lest they be hindered in their pursuit of perfect charity by the use of worldly things and by an adherence to riches which is contrary to the spirit of evangelical poverty (CCC 2545). Saint Francis of Assisi is the paradigm: born to a wealthy cloth merchant, he stripped himself before the bishop of Assisi, married "Lady Poverty," and founded an order that took the gospel call literally.

### 2. Blessed are those who mourn (Matthew 5:4)

**Promise:** they will be comforted | **Virtue cluster:** compassion, hope, faith | **Catechism:** CCC 1716, 2447

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted."

The mourning Christ blesses is not merely the grief of bereavement, though it includes that. It is also the godly sorrow Paul writes of (2 Corinthians 7:10): sorrow over sin, both one's own and the world's. Christ promises consolation, not by removing the cross but by being present in it. The Catechism includes spiritual works of mercy that flow from this Beatitude — to comfort the sorrowful, to bear wrongs patiently, to pray for the living and the dead (CCC 2447). Saint Monica, who wept for years over her son Augustine's dissolute life, is the perennial witness: her tears, Ambrose told her, would not be lost. Augustine became one of the giants of Christian theology and traced his conversion to her perseverance.

### 3. Blessed are the meek (Matthew 5:5)

**Promise:** they will inherit the earth | **Virtue cluster:** meekness, patience, fortitude | **Catechism:** CCC 1716, 736

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth."

Meekness in Scripture is not weakness. It is the strength of one who has the power to retaliate and chooses not to — strength bridled by love. Christ describes himself as meek and humble of heart (Matthew 11:29). The promise echoes Psalm 37:11, which the Beatitude takes up and transposes from temporal possession of the land to the eschatological inheritance of the renewed creation. The Catechism names meekness among the fruits of the Spirit (CCC 736; cf. Galatians 5:22–23). Saint Francis de Sales, a seventeenth-century bishop and Doctor of the Church, taught that nothing is so strong as gentleness, and nothing so gentle as real strength. He won back tens of thousands of Calvinists in the Chablais not by argument alone but by patient, unfailing meekness in the face of insult.

### 4. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness (Matthew 5:6)

**Promise:** they will be filled | **Virtue cluster:** justice, zeal, hope | **Catechism:** CCC 1718, 2518

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they will be filled."

The Greek word translated "righteousness" (*dikaiosynē*) carries the double sense of right relationship with God and right relationship with neighbor. Christ promises that those whose desire for both is intense and persistent will be filled — not by their own effort but by grace. The Catechism teaches that righteousness, which is to say holiness, is one of the gifts of the Holy Spirit, who himself satisfies the longing he creates (CCC 1718, 2518). Saint Catherine of Siena hungered for the renewal of the Church to such an extent that she crossed Italy to confront the pope at Avignon and to demand his return to Rome. Her Dialogue, dictated in ecstasy, remains one of the great mystical works of the Catholic tradition.

### 5. Blessed are the merciful (Matthew 5:7)

**Promise:** they will receive mercy | **Virtue cluster:** mercy, forgiveness, charity | **Catechism:** CCC 2447, 2840

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are the merciful, for they will receive mercy."

Mercy is love that responds to misery. The Catechism teaches that the works of mercy are charitable actions by which we come to the aid of our neighbor in his spiritual and bodily necessities (CCC 2447), and that the petition of the Our Father — forgive us our trespasses as we forgive those who trespass against us — places a single condition on the divine mercy we receive: that we have first extended mercy to others (CCC 2838–2840). Saint Maria Goretti, eleven years old, dying of stab wounds inflicted by a young man who had attempted to assault her, was asked whether she forgave him. She said: I forgive him with all my heart, and I want him to be with me in paradise. He converted in prison years later and was present at her canonization.

### 6. Blessed are the pure in heart (Matthew 5:8)

**Promise:** they will see God | **Virtue cluster:** chastity, integrity, single-mindedness | **Catechism:** CCC 2517–2519

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they will see God."

Purity of heart is broader than sexual purity, though it includes it. The Catechism teaches that the sixth Beatitude proclaims, "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God." Pure in heart refers to those who have attuned their intellects and wills to the demands of God's holiness, chiefly in three areas: charity, chastity or sexual rectitude, love of truth and orthodoxy of faith. Purity of heart is the precondition of the vision of God; even now it enables us to see according to God, to accept others as neighbors; it lets us perceive the human body — ours and our neighbor's — as a temple of the Holy Spirit, a manifestation of divine beauty (CCC 2517–2519). Saint Agnes, twelve years old, refused marriage to the son of a Roman prefect, having consecrated her virginity to Christ, and gave her life rather than betray that consecration.

### 7. Blessed are the peacemakers (Matthew 5:9)

**Promise:** they will be called children of God | **Virtue cluster:** peace, justice, charity | **Catechism:** CCC 2302–2306

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God."

The peace Christ blesses is not the absence of conflict but the right ordering of human relationships under God — *shalom* in its fullness. Peacemaking is therefore active. It requires the courage to confront injustice, to seek reconciliation across hostile lines, and to refuse the violence that masquerades as strength. The Catechism teaches that peace cannot be attained on earth without safeguarding the goods of persons, free communication among men, respect for the dignity of persons and peoples, and the assiduous practice of fraternity (CCC 2304). Saint John Paul II, who had lived under both Nazi occupation and Soviet communism, taught at Assisi in 1986 that the future of peace depends on the conversion of hearts — a position consonant with the Beatitude's claim that peacemakers will be called children of the Father whose nature is peace.

### 8. Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake (Matthew 5:10)

**Promise:** the kingdom of heaven | **Virtue cluster:** fortitude, faith, perseverance | **Catechism:** CCC 1716, 2473–2474

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."

The first and the eighth Beatitudes share the same promise: theirs is the kingdom of heaven. The Beatitudes thus form an inclusio — they begin and end with the kingdom — and the persecuted are those who have received it most fully. The Catechism teaches that martyrdom is the supreme witness given to the truth of the faith: it means bearing witness even unto death; the martyr bears witness to Christ who died and rose, to whom he is united by charity (CCC 2473). Saint Maximilian Kolbe, a Polish Franciscan in Auschwitz, stepped forward to take the place of a condemned prisoner who cried out for his wife and children. He died in the starvation bunker on 14 August 1941. John Paul II canonized him in 1982 as a martyr of charity.

### Note on Matthew 5:11–12

> "Blessed are you when people revile you and persecute you and utter all kinds of evil against you falsely on my account. Rejoice and be glad, for your reward is great in heaven, for in the same way they persecuted the prophets who were before you."

Some commentators count this as a ninth Beatitude. The Church reads it as an extension of the eighth, addressing the disciples directly and shifting from the third-person blessing ("blessed are those who…") to the second person ("blessed are you when…"). It binds the persecuted Christian to the prophetic line of Israel and promises a heavenly reward proportionate to the suffering endured for Christ's name.

## The Lukan Beatitudes and Woes (Luke 6:20–26)

Luke records the same teaching in what is traditionally called the Sermon on the Plain. The Lukan version is shorter — four blessings — and pairs each blessing with a corresponding woe. The Lukan Beatitudes are also more concrete: the "poor" rather than the "poor in spirit," the "hungry" rather than those who hunger for righteousness.

**Scripture (NRSVCE):**
> "Blessed are you who are poor, for yours is the kingdom of God.
> Blessed are you who are hungry now, for you will be filled.
> Blessed are you who weep now, for you will laugh.
> Blessed are you when people hate you, and when they exclude you, revile you, and defame you on account of the Son of Man. Rejoice in that day and leap for joy, for surely your reward is great in heaven; for that is what their ancestors did to the prophets.
> But woe to you who are rich, for you have received your consolation.
> Woe to you who are full now, for you will be hungry.
> Woe to you who are laughing now, for you will mourn and weep.
> Woe to you when all speak well of you, for that is what their ancestors did to the false prophets."

The two accounts are complementary, not contradictory. Matthew's "poor in spirit" and Luke's "you who are poor" are both true: God blesses both interior detachment and the literal poor whom the world overlooks. The four Lukan woes are not curses pronounced by God but lamentations over those whose comforts foreclose their hunger for him.

## The Beatitudes and the Christian Life

The Beatitudes confront us with decisive moral choices. They invite us to purify our hearts of bad instincts and to seek the love of God above all else (CCC 1723). They teach us that true happiness is not found in riches or well-being, in human fame or power, or in any human achievement — however beneficial it may be — such as science, technology, and art, or indeed in any creature, but in God alone, the source of every good and of all love (CCC 1723). The decisive choice the Beatitudes set before us is between holding fast to the goods that the world prizes and clinging to the one Good who alone can satisfy.

The Beatitudes are not a series of additional commandments; they are the ground out of which the obedient love of God grows. They cannot be lived without the help of grace, which alone makes possible what they describe. Christian beatitude is therefore wholly a gift — and one that requires our cooperation, expressed in lives ordered to love of God and neighbor (Matthew 22:36–40).

## The Two Great Commandments

When asked which is the greatest commandment of the Law, Jesus replied:

> "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your mind. This is the greatest and first commandment. And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. On these two commandments hang all the law and the prophets." (Matthew 22:37–40, NRSVCE)

The Beatitudes describe the shape of a life that fulfills these two commandments. They are the portrait of one who loves God with whole heart and neighbor as self — the portrait, finally, of Christ himself.
