# Saint Simon the Zealot (d. c. 65)

### Saint Simon the Zealot (d. c. 65)

**Feast day:** October 28 (with Jude) | **Age at death:** unknown | **Martyrdom:** tradition has him martyred with Jude in Persia (sometimes Beirut, sometimes Britain in alternative traditions; the Persian account is best attested)
**Symbol in iconography:** a saw (the instrument of his martyrdom, in some traditions); a fish (his original calling); a book
**Known for:** the only apostle from the Zealot party (or so the epithet has long been understood)

Simon is called "the Cananaean" by Matthew and Mark (Matthew 10:4; Mark 3:18) and "the Zealot" by Luke (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13). The Cananaean designation is the Aramaic equivalent of the Greek *Zealot*; both refer either to the Zealot political faction (who sought armed revolt against Rome) or, more broadly, to a man of zealous religious temperament. Beyond his inclusion in the apostolic lists, the New Testament gives no narrative concerning him. Tradition unites him with Jude Thaddeus in a Persian mission and martyrdom; their feast day is shared. His witness in the apostolic college is notable for one reason: he stands beside Matthew the tax collector. The Zealots saw the tax collectors as the worst kind of collaborators with Rome; Matthew would have seen the Zealots as fanatics. That Christ called both, and that both followed him, is itself an early image of the unity Christ creates across irreconcilable human divisions.
