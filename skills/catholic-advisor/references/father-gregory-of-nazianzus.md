# Saint Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 329 – c. 390)

### Saint Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 329 – c. 390)

**Era:** Greek (Cappadocian) | **Feast day:** January 2 (with Basil) | **Age at death:** approximately 61
**Doctor of the Church:** Yes (proclaimed 1568); known in the East as *Gregory the Theologian* — a title given by the Byzantine tradition to only three: John the Evangelist, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Symeon the New Theologian
**Major works:** the Five Theological Orations (delivered at Constantinople in 380); poems; many letters
**Known for:** the foremost theological orator of the patristic era; briefly Archbishop of Constantinople; presided briefly over Constantinople I (381)

Gregory was the lifelong friend of Basil and a thinker of remarkable subtlety. The Five Theological Orations are masterpieces of Trinitarian theology, defending the consubstantiality of the Son and the divinity of the Holy Spirit against the residual Arian and pneumatomachian opposition. He was raised to the see of Constantinople in 379 to lead the defense of orthodoxy in a city dominated by Arians. He presided over the early sessions of the First Council of Constantinople in 381, but the politics of the council were brutal and he resigned to return to his small Cappadocian estate at Arianzus, where he spent his last years writing poetry. His phrase "*what has not been assumed has not been healed*" (*Epistle* 101) — the principle that Christ must have assumed every aspect of human nature in order to save it — is the touchstone of orthodox Christology against Apollinarianism.
