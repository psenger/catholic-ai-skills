# Clement of Alexandria (c. 150 – c. 215)

### Clement of Alexandria (c. 150 – c. 215)

**Era:** Greek (Alexandrian) | **Feast day:** removed from the Roman calendar in 1586; he is not venerated by the Catholic Church (though he is honored by some Eastern Christians)
**Doctor of the Church:** No
**Major works:** *Protrepticus* (Exhortation to the Greeks); *Paedagogus* (The Instructor); *Stromata* (Miscellanies)
**Known for:** the first head of the catechetical school of Alexandria; the theologian who argued most extensively that philosophy was a preparation for the Gospel among the Greeks

Clement was a convert, probably from paganism. He taught in Alexandria, the intellectual capital of the late ancient world, and is the first known director of the catechetical school there — the school in which young Origen would soon be trained. Clement was a theologian of culture: he held that Greek philosophy was a *paidagogos* (a tutor) for the Greeks as the Law was for the Jews, preparing both peoples for the Gospel. His writings are vast and learned, full of citations from Homer and Plato as well as from Scripture. His omission from the Roman Martyrology in 1586 (under Sixtus V, on Baronius's advice) reflects later doubts about certain of his theological positions; he is nevertheless universally regarded as a Father of the Church.
