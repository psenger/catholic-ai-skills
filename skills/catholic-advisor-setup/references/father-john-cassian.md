# Saint John Cassian (c. 360 – c. 435)

### Saint John Cassian (c. 360 – c. 435)

**Era:** Latin | **Feast day:** July 23 in Eastern calendars; he is not in the universal Roman calendar but is venerated locally (especially at Marseille)
**Doctor of the Church:** No
**Major works:** *Institutes* (on the rules of monastic life); *Conferences* (twenty-four extended dialogues on the spiritual life)
**Known for:** the principal Latin transmitter of Eastern monastic spirituality to the West; the source on which Benedict drew when writing the Rule

Cassian spent years in the deserts of Egypt and Palestine learning from the great desert monks. He brought their teaching westward to Marseille, where he founded two monasteries (one for men, one for women) around 415. His *Institutes* set out the practical rules of monastic life; his *Conferences* record extended conversations with the most spiritually mature desert fathers. Benedict's Rule directs that the *Conferences* be read in community after Vespers each day. Cassian's status in the Catholic tradition is slightly clouded by his role in the "semi-Pelagian" controversies — he was uneasy with some of Augustine's positions on grace — but he is universally received as a Father and as the indispensable bridge between Eastern monasticism and the Latin West.
