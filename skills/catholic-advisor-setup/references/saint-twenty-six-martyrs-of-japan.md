# The Twenty-Six Martyrs of Japan (Saint Paul Miki and Companions, d. 1597)

### The Twenty-Six Martyrs of Japan (Saint Paul Miki and Companions, d. 1597)

**Feast day:** February 6 | **Ages at death:** various (the youngest, Louis Ibaraki, was 12)
**Canonization status:** Saints (canonized 1862 by Pius IX)
**Known for:** twenty-six Catholics — six Franciscan missionaries (four Spanish, one Mexican, one Indian), three Japanese Jesuits, and seventeen Japanese laymen — crucified at Nagasaki on February 5, 1597

Christianity had been brought to Japan by Francis Xavier in 1549; by the late sixteenth century there were several hundred thousand Japanese Catholics. The Toyotomi Hideyoshi regime turned against the Christians in 1587, and the long persecution that followed produced tens of thousands of martyrs. The twenty-six executed at Nagasaki in 1597 were marched on foot for hundreds of miles from Kyoto, had their left ears partially severed in mockery, and were crucified on a hill outside Nagasaki. The twelve-year-old Louis Ibaraki, asked by a passing samurai if he would like to go free, answered that he would rather die with the others.

**Sources:** Vatican.va canonization documents; Catholic Encyclopedia entry on Japanese Martyrs
