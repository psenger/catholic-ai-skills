# Judas Iscariot (d. 30 or 33)

### Judas Iscariot (d. 30 or 33)

**Feast day:** none — Judas is not venerated | **Age at death:** unknown | **Death:** Matthew records that he hanged himself (Matthew 27:5); Acts records that he fell headlong and his body burst open (Acts 1:18); the two accounts have traditionally been harmonized as describing different stages or aspects of the same event
**Symbol in iconography:** he is included in Last Supper iconography (often clutching a money bag) but receives no devotional symbol of his own
**Known for:** the betrayer; the cautionary figure of the apostolic college

Judas of Kerioth is included in every list of the Twelve, always last, and always with the identifying clause "who also betrayed him" (Matthew 10:4; Mark 3:19; Luke 6:16). He kept the common purse (John 12:6) and is described in John as a thief. He bargained with the chief priests for thirty pieces of silver (Matthew 26:14–16), led the arresting party to Gethsemane, and identified Jesus with a kiss (Matthew 26:47–50). Matthew records that when he saw Jesus condemned, he "repented and brought back the thirty pieces of silver… and he went and hanged himself" (Matthew 27:3–5). Acts gives a different account of his death (Acts 1:18). The Church has not pronounced any judgment on Judas's eternal state — and indeed teaches that she has never made a final judgment on any particular person's damnation, leaving such judgment entirely to the mercy of God (CCC 1037). Judas is included in this file not for veneration but because the apostolic record cannot be told without him, and because his fall remains the sharpest reminder that proximity to Christ does not, by itself, guarantee fidelity.
