# The Church Fathers: Reference for the Catholic Advisor Skill

This reference supports the `catholic-advisor` skill. It collects the Apostolic Fathers, the Greek (Eastern) Fathers, and the Latin (Western) Fathers — the earliest Christian writers whose lives and works the Catholic Church recognizes as foundational witnesses to apostolic teaching. Dates, major works, doctrinal positions, and Doctor of the Church status have been cross-checked against the Catholic Encyclopedia (newadvent.org) and the broader patrological tradition. The texts of nearly every Father below are available in English translation in newadvent.org's *Fathers* collection.

## What Makes a Church Father

The Church recognizes a person as a Father of the Church when four conditions are met (the four classical criteria of Vincent of Lérins, refined in modern patrology): *antiquity* — the Father lived in the patristic era (roughly the first eight centuries); *orthodoxy of doctrine* — his teaching is in conformity with apostolic faith as the Church holds it; *holiness of life* — he is venerated as a holy man; *ecclesiastical approval* — the Church receives his writings as a faithful witness to Tradition.

The patristic age is traditionally bounded in the East by John of Damascus (d. 749) and in the West by Isidore of Seville (d. 636) or Bede the Venerable (d. 735), depending on the patrologist. Beyond these dates, theologians who continue to deepen the faith are called *Doctors of the Church* — a broader category that includes the Fathers but also later figures such as Aquinas, Bonaventure, Catherine of Siena, and Thérèse of Lisieux. Every Father of the Church who is also a Doctor is marked below.

## How the Skill Should Use This File

When a question concerns a Father of the Church, an early Christian writing (Confessions, City of God, Against Heresies, the Didache, the letters of Ignatius, the catecheses of Cyril of Jerusalem), the doctrinal controversies of the first eight centuries (Arianism, Pelagianism, Gnosticism, Monophysitism, Iconoclasm), apostolic succession, or the early development of Catholic doctrine, consult this file.

## Apostolic Fathers

These are the writers of the late first and early second century who knew at least one apostle personally or stood within the first generation of the apostles' disciples.
