# 16. Council of Constance (1414–1418)

### 16. Council of Constance (1414–1418)

**Convoked by:** the antipope John XXIII; reconvened and ratified by Pope Gregory XII's resignation and the election of Pope Martin V
**Issue addressed:** the Western Schism — the period (since 1378) during which two and then three claimants to the papacy reigned simultaneously, dividing Catholic loyalty across the kingdoms of Europe
**Key teachings:**
- Ended the Western Schism by accepting Gregory XII's resignation, deposing the other claimants, and electing Martin V
- Condemned the teachings of John Wycliffe (posthumously) and Jan Hus (who was tried at the council and burned at the stake — a violation of the safe-conduct that the Emperor Sigismund had promised him)
- Issued the decree *Haec Sancta* (1415), claiming the supremacy of a general council over the pope — a "conciliarist" claim that the subsequent Magisterium has never accepted as authoritative

Constance was a council called to solve an institutional crisis, and at that level it succeeded: it ended the Schism. But its conciliarist excesses (*Haec Sancta* and *Frequens*) are not received as binding by the Catholic Church, which holds that the pope cannot be deposed by a council; only papal ratification gives council decrees their binding character. The execution of Jan Hus is a permanent stain. In 1999 Pope John Paul II expressed deep regret for the way Hus was treated.
