## Origin and Authority of the Decalogue

The Ten Commandments — *Decalogue*, literally "ten words" (Exodus 34:28; Deuteronomy 4:13) — were given by God to Moses on Mount Sinai as the heart of his covenant with Israel. They are also recorded a second time in Deuteronomy 5:6–21 as Moses recalls the Sinai event to the second generation about to enter the Promised Land. The Catechism teaches that the Decalogue contains a privileged expression of the natural law and makes known to us with divine authority the unchanging principles of the moral law (CCC 2070–2071). It is therefore binding on every human being — not merely on Israel or on Christians, but on every rational creature, because it makes explicit what conscience already grasps obscurely.

Jesus did not abolish the Decalogue. To the rich young man who asked what he must do to inherit eternal life, Jesus said: "If you wish to enter into life, keep the commandments" (Matthew 19:17). He then deepened the commandments by tracing them to their interior root — anger as the seed of murder, lust as the seed of adultery (Matthew 5:21–30). He summarized the whole Law in the two Great Commandments: love of God and love of neighbor (Matthew 22:37–40), which together "hang all the law and the prophets." The Decalogue is the concrete spelling-out of what love of God and love of neighbor look like in practice.

## Catholic Enumeration

The Catholic Church and the Lutheran tradition follow Saint Augustine's division of the Decalogue, in which the prohibition of false gods and the prohibition of graven images together form the First Commandment, and the prohibition of coveting is split into the Ninth (the neighbor's wife) and the Tenth (the neighbor's goods). The Reformed Protestant and Eastern Orthodox traditions follow a different division in which the prohibition of images is the Second Commandment and the prohibition of coveting forms a single Tenth. The underlying moral content is identical; only the numbering differs. The Catechism explains the Catholic enumeration in CCC 2066.

The Decalogue has a threefold structure (CCC 2067): the first three commandments govern the duties owed to God; the remaining seven govern the duties owed to neighbor. This structure reflects the order of the two Great Commandments — God first, neighbor second — and shows that the moral life is, at root, the life of love.

## The Ten Commandments

### 1. First Commandment: I am the Lord your God; you shall worship no other gods (Exodus 20:2–6)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery; you shall have no other gods before me. You shall not make for yourself an idol, whether in the form of anything that is in heaven above, or that is on the earth beneath, or that is in the water under the earth. You shall not bow down to them or worship them; for I the Lord your God am a jealous God, punishing children for the iniquity of parents, to the third and the fourth generation of those who reject me, but showing steadfast love to the thousandth generation of those who love me and keep my commandments."

**Catechism:** CCC 2084–2141 | **Positive call:** to adore God alone, to render him the worship of latria, to live by the theological virtues of faith, hope, and charity
**Matter addressed:** atheism, idolatry (including the idolatry of money, power, ideology, and self), superstition, divination and magic, sacrilege, simony, indifferentism, despair, presumption, hatred of God

The first commandment summons the human person to the only worship that befits him — adoration of the God who has revealed himself. The Catechism teaches that the first commandment embraces faith, hope, and charity, the three theological virtues that bind us directly to God (CCC 2086). To make a creature into a god — whether a literal idol, an ideology, money, the self, or even a legitimate good held disordinately — is to falsify the relationship of creature to Creator. The Catechism also teaches that the veneration of images of Christ, of the Virgin, and of the saints is not contrary to this commandment but is a continuation of the Incarnation, by which the invisible God became visible in his Son (CCC 2129–2132; cf. Second Council of Nicaea, 787). Sins against this commandment include atheism, agnosticism, divination, the deliberate testing of God, sacrilege, and simony. The positive call is the worship of God in spirit and in truth.

### 2. Second Commandment: You shall not take the name of the Lord your God in vain (Exodus 20:7)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "You shall not make wrongful use of the name of the Lord your God, for the Lord will not acquit anyone who misuses his name."

**Catechism:** CCC 2142–2167 | **Positive call:** to honor God's name in praise, in prayer, in oath when required, in the names given to children at baptism
**Matter addressed:** blasphemy, perjury, false oaths, the misuse of God's name in cursing or in trivial speech, the abuse of holy names in mockery

The name of God is holy because it is God himself in his self-revelation. To name him is to enter a relationship with him; to misuse his name is to wound that relationship. The Catechism teaches that respect for the name of God is an expression of the respect owed to God himself (CCC 2144). Blasphemy — words, thoughts, or actions of hatred, reproach, or defiance directed against God — is gravely sinful by its nature (CCC 2148). Perjury, the calling of God to witness a lie, is grave because it implicates God in the lie (CCC 2150–2152). The Christian's name at baptism is itself a participation in the holiness of God's name; this is why the Church directs that names given at baptism be Christian (CCC 2156–2159). The positive call is to a habit of reverent speech, a tongue trained to bless and not to curse.

### 3. Third Commandment: Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy (Exodus 20:8–11)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "Remember the sabbath day, and keep it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work. But the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God; you shall not do any work — you, your son or your daughter, your male or female slave, your livestock, or the alien resident in your towns. For in six days the Lord made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that is in them, but rested the seventh day; therefore the Lord blessed the sabbath day and consecrated it."

**Catechism:** CCC 2168–2195 | **Positive call:** to keep Sunday — the Lord's Day — holy through participation in the Eucharist and abstention from servile work
**Matter addressed:** missing Sunday Mass through one's own fault (grave matter, CCC 2181), failure to rest, the reduction of Sunday to a day of commerce or unrelieved labor

The sabbath of the Old Covenant marked the seventh day, when God rested from creation. For Christians, the day of the Lord is Sunday — the day of Christ's Resurrection — which both fulfills the sabbath (Christ enters his rest by entering glory) and inaugurates the new creation. The Catechism teaches that Sunday is the foremost holy day of obligation in the universal Church (CCC 2177) and that the faithful are obliged to participate in the Mass on Sundays and holy days of obligation. Deliberately failing to fulfill this obligation is a grave sin (CCC 2181). Sunday also requires rest from servile work and a deliberate cultivation of family life, prayer, and works of mercy (CCC 2184–2188). The third commandment thus binds together worship and rest: the human being is made not only for labor but for the joy of contemplating God.

### 4. Fourth Commandment: Honor your father and your mother (Exodus 20:12)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "Honor your father and your mother, so that your days may be long in the land that the Lord your God is giving you."

**Catechism:** CCC 2197–2257 | **Positive call:** to honor, obey, support, and pray for one's parents; by extension, to honor all legitimate authority and to exercise authority in service
**Matter addressed:** filial respect, the duties of parents, the duties of citizens, the duties of civil authorities, conscientious objection, the limits of obedience, the family as the original cell of social life

The fourth commandment is the hinge between the duties to God and the duties to neighbor. It is the first commandment with a promise (Ephesians 6:2–3) and the first to govern human relationships. The Catechism teaches that the family is the original cell of social life and that the fourth commandment "shows us the order of charity," requiring us to honor those through whom we have received life (CCC 2207, 2197). It binds children to parents not only in childhood but for life, including the duty to support aging parents (CCC 2218). It also imposes serious duties on parents — to provide for the material and spiritual needs of their children, to educate them in the faith, to respect their children as persons (CCC 2221–2231). By extension, the commandment governs every relationship of legitimate authority: in the workplace, in civil society, in the Church. The Catechism teaches that the citizen is obliged in conscience not to follow the directives of civil authorities when they are contrary to the demands of the moral order, to the fundamental rights of persons, or to the teachings of the Gospel (CCC 2242).

### 5. Fifth Commandment: You shall not murder (Exodus 20:13)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "You shall not murder."

**Catechism:** CCC 2258–2330 | **Positive call:** to respect and protect human life from conception to natural death; to safeguard one's own life and health; to make peace
**Matter addressed:** murder (intrinsically and gravely evil), abortion (CCC 2270–2275, grave matter, latae sententiae excommunication for the act of formal cooperation), euthanasia (CCC 2276–2279, grave matter), suicide (CCC 2280–2283, while final judgment belongs to God who knows the conditions reducing culpability), legitimate defense (CCC 2263–2267), capital punishment (CCC 2267 as revised by Pope Francis in 2018, declaring it inadmissible), just war (CCC 2307–2317), care of the body, scandal

The fifth commandment is rooted in the doctrine of human dignity: every human being, from the moment of conception until natural death, bears the image of God (Genesis 1:27; cf. CCC 2258). The Catechism teaches that human life is sacred because from its beginning it involves the creative action of God and it remains for ever in a special relationship with the Creator, who is its sole end. God alone is the Lord of life from its beginning until its end: no one can under any circumstance claim for himself the right directly to destroy an innocent human being (CCC 2258). The Church's teaching against abortion and euthanasia is therefore unchangeable: direct abortion, willed as an end or means, is gravely contrary to the moral law (CCC 2271); euthanasia is morally unacceptable (CCC 2277). The Catechism also addresses legitimate self-defense, just war, and capital punishment. In 2018 Pope Francis revised paragraph 2267 of the Catechism to teach that the death penalty is inadmissible because it is an attack on the inviolability and dignity of the person, and the Church works for its abolition worldwide.

### 6. Sixth Commandment: You shall not commit adultery (Exodus 20:14)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "You shall not commit adultery."

**Catechism:** CCC 2331–2400 | **Positive call:** to live the chastity proper to one's state of life (married, single, consecrated); to honor the goods of marriage — fidelity, indissolubility, openness to life
**Matter addressed:** adultery, fornication, masturbation, pornography, prostitution, rape, homosexual acts, contraception (Humanae Vitae 1968), divorce (when contrary to the indissoluble bond), polygamy, the gravity of incest, the dignity of marriage as a covenant raised by Christ to the dignity of a sacrament

The sixth commandment protects the integrity of marriage and the integrity of the human person as a sexual being. The Catechism teaches that sexuality affects all aspects of the human person in the unity of his body and soul; it especially concerns affectivity, the capacity to love and to procreate, and in a more general way the aptitude for forming bonds of communion with others (CCC 2332). Chastity is the integration of sexuality within the person; it requires the apprenticeship of self-mastery (CCC 2337–2345). Christ deepened the commandment by tracing it to the interior disposition: "Everyone who looks at a woman with lust has already committed adultery with her in his heart" (Matthew 5:27–28; CCC 2336). The Catechism treats specific matters under this commandment with theological precision: lust (2351), masturbation (2352), fornication (2353), pornography (2354), prostitution (2355), rape (2356), and homosexual acts (2357–2359 — distinguishing the inclination from acts, calling persons with this inclination to chastity and treating them with respect, compassion, and sensitivity). The Catechism affirms the indissolubility of marriage (CCC 2360–2400) and the openness of every conjugal act to the transmission of life (Humanae Vitae 11; CCC 2366).

### 7. Seventh Commandment: You shall not steal (Exodus 20:15)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "You shall not steal."

**Catechism:** CCC 2401–2463 | **Positive call:** to respect the property of others, to honor commitments and contracts, to give what is just in commerce, to practice solidarity, to care for creation, to give alms
**Matter addressed:** theft (CCC 2408), fraud, willful retention of borrowed items, business practices that harm the common good, paying unjust wages (CCC 2434), tax evasion, work and the dignity of workers, the universal destination of goods, the legitimacy of private property, just stewardship of creation (the basis for Catholic environmental teaching, see *Laudato Si'*), the duty to give alms, the social doctrine of the Church

The seventh commandment forbids unjustly taking or keeping the goods of one's neighbor and wronging him in any way with respect to his goods (CCC 2401). The Catechism teaches that all created goods are destined by God for the benefit of all humanity (universal destination of goods) and that the right to private property, while real and important, is subordinated to this universal destination (CCC 2402–2406). The Catholic social doctrine flowing from this commandment is among the richest portions of the Catechism: it addresses just wages, the rights of workers, the licit purposes of strikes, the moral evaluation of economic systems, the obligations of the rich nations toward the poor (CCC 2426–2436), the duty of solidarity (CCC 2437–2442), and the love of the poor as a constitutive demand of the gospel (CCC 2443–2449). The seventh commandment thus becomes the gateway to the entire Catholic social tradition, from Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) through Francis's *Laudato Si'* (2015) and *Fratelli Tutti* (2020).

### 8. Eighth Commandment: You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor (Exodus 20:16)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor."

**Catechism:** CCC 2464–2513 | **Positive call:** to live in truthfulness, to bear witness to Christ even at the cost of suffering, to keep secrets that should be kept, to preserve the good name of one's neighbor
**Matter addressed:** lying (CCC 2482–2486 — defined as speech contrary to the truth uttered with the intention to deceive), perjury, calumny (lying that damages another's reputation), detraction (revealing without good reason the faults of another), rash judgment, gossip, the seal of confession, professional secrets, the duties of the media to truth

The eighth commandment forbids misrepresenting the truth in our relations with others (CCC 2464). The Catechism teaches that truthfulness keeps to the just mean between what ought to be expressed and what ought to be kept secret: it entails honesty and discretion (CCC 2469). Lying is the most direct offense against the truth; the Catechism defines a lie as speaking a falsehood with the intention of deceiving (CCC 2482). The gravity of a lie is measured by the truth it deforms, the circumstances, the intentions, and the harm suffered by its victims (CCC 2484). The commandment is also a positive summons to bear witness — *martyrein* in Greek — to the truth of Christ, even unto death; this is the supreme witness of the martyr (CCC 2473). The seal of confession is inviolable (CCC 2490). The eighth commandment regulates also the responsibilities of the communications media, who are obliged to serve truth, justice, and the common good (CCC 2493–2499).

### 9. Ninth Commandment: You shall not covet your neighbor's wife (Exodus 20:17a)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "You shall not covet your neighbor's house; you shall not covet your neighbor's wife..."

**Catechism:** CCC 2514–2533 | **Positive call:** to purify the heart, to live the modesty proper to one's state, to discipline the imagination and the eyes
**Matter addressed:** concupiscence of the flesh (lust as an interior disposition), the purification of the heart, the battle for chastity, modesty in dress and behavior, the formation of the imagination

The ninth commandment moves the moral law inward, naming the disorder of desire that precedes the sinful act. Where the sixth commandment forbids the act of adultery, the ninth forbids carnal concupiscence — the interior disposition that, unmastered, gives rise to such acts. The Catechism teaches that the heart is the seat of moral personality: the pure of heart are those who have attuned their intellects and wills to the demands of God's holiness (CCC 2518). The battle for purity of heart involves prayer, chastity, purity of intention and of vision, the cultivation of modesty (CCC 2520, 2521–2527). The Catechism is candid that the human being is wounded by original sin: the disorder of concupiscence is not itself sin but inclines to sin, and the Christian life is in part a sustained struggle against this disorder, sustained by grace (CCC 2515–2516). Modesty is named as the proper "guardian" of chastity, protecting the intimacy of the person (CCC 2521–2522).

### 10. Tenth Commandment: You shall not covet your neighbor's goods (Exodus 20:17b)

**Biblical text (NRSVCE — verified):**
> "...you shall not covet your neighbor's house; you shall not covet your neighbor's wife, or male or female slave, or ox, or donkey, or anything that belongs to your neighbor."

**Catechism:** CCC 2534–2557 | **Positive call:** to detachment from riches, to trust in providence, to gratitude, to almsgiving, to the poverty of spirit that opens the heart to the Beatitudes
**Matter addressed:** greed and avarice (CCC 2536), envy (CCC 2538–2540), inordinate ambition, the inversion of means and ends that turns money or possessions into idols, the spiritual ascesis of detachment

The tenth commandment, like the ninth, moves the moral law inward. It forbids not merely the act of theft (already condemned by the seventh) but the disordered desire that would lead to it. The Catechism teaches that the tenth commandment forbids greed and the desire to amass earthly goods without limit; it forbids avarice arising from a passion for riches and their attendant power; it also forbids the desire to commit injustice by harming our neighbor in his temporal goods (CCC 2536). Envy is named with particular seriousness: it is sadness at the sight of another's goods and the immoderate desire to acquire them for oneself (CCC 2539); it is a capital sin and one of the works of the flesh (CCC 2540). The tenth commandment circles back to the first Beatitude: blessed are the poor in spirit. The remedy for covetousness is not asceticism for its own sake but the conviction that God himself is our true good and that no creature can substitute for him (CCC 2547–2550).

## The Two Great Commandments

When asked which is the greatest commandment, Jesus answered (Matthew 22:34–40, NRSVCE):

> "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your mind. This is the greatest and first commandment. And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. On these two commandments hang all the law and the prophets."

The Decalogue is not a list of arbitrary prohibitions. It is the concrete shape of what love of God (commandments 1–3) and love of neighbor (commandments 4–10) require. To keep the commandments is not to earn love but to live in the love already given. The Catechism teaches that the Decalogue must be interpreted in light of this double yet single commandment of love, the fullness of the Law (CCC 2055).

## Sin and the Commandments

The Catechism distinguishes between mortal and venial sin. Mortal sin requires three conditions simultaneously: grave matter, full knowledge of the moral disorder of the act, and deliberate consent (CCC 1857–1859). The Decalogue specifies the grave matter; the commandments are therefore the standard against which serious matter is measured. Venial sin is committed when, in a less serious matter, one does not observe the standard prescribed by the moral law, or when one disobeys the moral law in a grave matter but without full knowledge or without complete consent (CCC 1862). The commandments are also the path of return: the sacrament of Reconciliation restores the soul to friendship with God when sin has ruptured it (CCC 1422–1498).
