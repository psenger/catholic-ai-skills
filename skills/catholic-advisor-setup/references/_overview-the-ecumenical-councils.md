# The Ecumenical Councils: Reference for the Catholic Advisor Skill

This reference supports the `catholic-advisor` skill. It covers all twenty-one councils that the Catholic Church recognizes as ecumenical — the first seven of which are also recognized by the Eastern Orthodox Church, and the first four of which most ancient liturgical traditions (including the Oriental Orthodox) hold in honor. Dates, convoking popes, and major dogmatic and disciplinary acts have been cross-checked against the Catholic Encyclopedia (newadvent.org) and the Vatican's own councils archive (vatican.va).

## What Makes a Council Ecumenical

The Catechism teaches that an ecumenical council is "a college or body of bishops convened to discuss and decide questions of doctrine and discipline, the decrees of which, when confirmed by the pope, are binding on the universal Church." A council is ecumenical when (1) it is convoked by, or with the authorization of, the Bishop of Rome; (2) the bishops of the whole Church are summoned to take part; and (3) the pope ratifies its acts. When teaching definitively in such a council, the bishops with the pope exercise the Church's supreme and infallible Magisterium (CCC 884, 891). Not every act of a council is dogmatic: councils also legislate on discipline, on liturgy, and on pastoral life. The dogmatic definitions are irreformable; the disciplinary canons are subject to revision in later councils and in the universal Magisterium.

The Catholic count is twenty-one. The Eastern Orthodox accept only the first seven. The Council of Constantinople IV (869–870) is recognized by Rome but disputed in the East, where a competing later synod (879–880) is sometimes preferred. The "Robber Council" of Ephesus (449) is *not* counted because it was repudiated at Chalcedon two years later.

## How the Skill Should Use This File

When a question concerns a council, a dogmatic definition originating in a council (Nicene Creed, Theotokos, Chalcedonian Definition, transubstantiation, papal infallibility), a document from Vatican II (*Lumen Gentium*, *Dei Verbum*, *Sacrosanctum Concilium*, *Gaudium et Spes*, *Nostra Aetate*, *Dignitatis Humanae*), or the development of doctrine, consult this file. Each entry gives the date, the convoking pope, the principal issue, the principal teaching or canon, and the lasting significance.
