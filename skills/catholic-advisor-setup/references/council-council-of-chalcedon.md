# 4. Council of Chalcedon (451)

### 4. Council of Chalcedon (451)

**Convoked by:** Pope Leo I "the Great" (summoned by the Emperor Marcian) | **Bishops present:** approximately 500, the largest council to that time
**Heresy addressed:** Monophysitism (Eutychianism) — the teaching that Christ had only one nature (divine), in which his humanity was absorbed
**Key teachings:**
- The Chalcedonian Definition: Christ is "one and the same Son, perfect in divinity and perfect in humanity, true God and true man, the same of a rational soul and a body, consubstantial with the Father as to his divinity and consubstantial with us as to his humanity… acknowledged in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation"
- The Tome of Leo, sent by Pope Leo I to the council, was acclaimed as the standard of orthodox christology: "Peter has spoken through Leo"
- The patriarchate of Constantinople was assigned equal honor with Rome (Canon 28), a canon Pope Leo refused to ratify

Chalcedon is one of the four great councils Pope Gregory the Great compared to the four Gospels. Its Definition is the magna carta of orthodox christology — the formula that all subsequent councils presuppose. The four adverbs — without confusion, without change, without division, without separation — are the precise theological boundary against the heresies of every direction: against the Monophysites (who would confuse the natures), the Nestorians (who would divide them), the Apollinarians (who would change the humanity), and the Adoptionists (who would separate). The council's reception was traumatic in the East: large portions of the Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, and Ethiopian Churches rejected the Definition and remain (the Oriental Orthodox) outside communion with Rome and the Byzantine Orthodox to this day. Contemporary ecumenical dialogue has substantially clarified that the underlying Christology of the Oriental Orthodox is not strictly Monophysite, and joint declarations with Pope Paul VI, John Paul II, and Pope Francis have affirmed a common faith in the one Christ.
