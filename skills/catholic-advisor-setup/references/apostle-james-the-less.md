# Saint James the Less (d. c. 62)

### Saint James the Less (d. c. 62)

**Feast day:** May 3 (with Philip) | **Age at death:** unknown | **Martyrdom:** thrown from the Temple parapet in Jerusalem and clubbed to death, c. 62 (reported by Josephus and Hegesippus)
**Symbol in iconography:** a fuller's club (the instrument of his martyrdom)
**Known for:** "James the brother of the Lord"; first bishop of Jerusalem (in traditional identification); the Letter of James

The identification of James the Less, son of Alphaeus (named in the apostolic lists), with James "the brother of the Lord" (mentioned in Galatians 1:19, Mark 6:3, and elsewhere) and with the first bishop of Jerusalem (Acts 15) is a matter of long Catholic tradition associated especially with Jerome. The Eastern Orthodox tradition more often treats them as distinct, though related, figures. Scholarship continues to debate the matter. Whatever the precise relationships, the James who presided at the Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15) and whose Letter is the New Testament document most engaged with the practical demands of Christian life ("faith without works is dead," James 2:26) is among the great pillars of the apostolic Church. Josephus records that in AD 62, during the interregnum between Roman governors, the high priest Ananus had James condemned and stoned; Hegesippus adds that, having been thrown from the parapet of the Temple, he was finished off with a club. The word *Catholic* in the title "Catholic Epistle of James" simply means *general* — addressed to the Church as a whole.
