# 6. Third Council of Constantinople (680–681)

### 6. Third Council of Constantinople (680–681)

**Convoked by:** Pope Agatho (closed under Pope Leo II)
**Heresy addressed:** Monothelitism — the teaching that Christ had only one will (the divine), even though he had two natures
**Key teachings:**
- Defined two wills and two operations in Christ (one divine, one human), corresponding to his two natures
- Affirmed that the human will of Christ is in perfect submission to his divine will, without conflict between them
- Condemned Monothelitism

The council resolved the last great christological controversy of antiquity. If Christ is fully man, he must have a human will; otherwise his humanity is hollow. Maximus the Confessor, who died in 662 with his tongue cut out for refusing to accept Monothelitism, was vindicated. The council also condemned, embarrassingly for Rome, the seventh-century Pope Honorius I for having tolerated Monothelite language in his correspondence. This condemnation became a fixture in nineteenth-century arguments against papal infallibility; the standard Catholic response is that Honorius spoke as a private theologian, not as defining doctrine *ex cathedra*.
