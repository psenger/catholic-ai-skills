# youth-communication-coach

**Practical guidance for Catholic adults having hard conversations with the young people in their lives.**

The hardest conversations aren't the ones you don't know how to start — they're the ones where you start wrong and the door closes before you get to what matters.

`youth-communication-coach` helps Catholic parents, teachers, mentors, and youth ministers navigate those conversations. It gives age-specific language strategies, natural sentence openers, and real Catholic moral framing — not secular platitudes, not generic "just be open and honest" advice. The Beatitudes, the theological virtues, the Church's actual teaching on chastity, death, grief, and mercy: grounded guidance for the people doing the daily work of formation.

## Usage

Pass the age and situation directly:

```
/youth-communication-coach 9-year-old whose grandfather just died
```

```
/youth-communication-coach 16-year-old asking about dating and relationships
```

```
/youth-communication-coach 22-year-old who just made a serious moral failure
```

```
/youth-communication-coach teenager who is questioning the faith
```

Or invoke without arguments and the skill will ask what it needs:

```
/youth-communication-coach
```

## Catholic framework

This skill is explicitly Catholic. It does not offer a secular framework with Catholic notes bolted on.

**The Beatitudes** (Mt 5:3-12, CCC 1716-1724) anchor every difficult conversation. "Blessed are those who mourn" reshapes how a parent talks about death. "Blessed are the pure in heart" grounds the conversation about relationships in dignity rather than rules. "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness" is the honest thing to say to a teenager facing peer pressure — and it's more useful than anything generic.

**The theological virtues** — faith, hope, and charity — are supernatural gifts that elevate what natural virtue alone cannot sustain. A teenager in crisis does not need Stoic self-mastery; they need hope. A young adult who has fallen needs mercy. These are different things, and the skill treats them as different things.

**The cardinal virtues** — prudence, fortitude, justice, temperance — applied age-specifically from early childhood through young adulthood.

**The theology of the body** grounds the conversation about relationships and sexuality in the dignity of the person, not in a list of prohibitions.

**The communion of saints** changes everything about how a Catholic parent talks about death. "We can still pray for him, and ask him to pray for us" is not a comfort; it is the truth about how the Church is structured.

## One example

The secular approach to a 9-year-old asking about a grandparent's death:

> "Grandpa died today. That means his body stopped working and he won't be with us anymore. It's okay to feel sad."

The Catholic approach:

> "Grandpa died today. His body stopped working, but his soul — the part of him that loved us — is with God now. We can still pray for him, and we believe we'll see him again one day. It's okay to feel really sad. Even Jesus cried when his friend Lazarus died."

One answer ends at the grave. The other opens a door into the whole faith.
