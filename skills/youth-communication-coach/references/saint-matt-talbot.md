# Venerable Matt Talbot (1856–1925)

**Feast day:** June 19 | **Beatitude(s):** Poor in spirit, meek | **Virtue(s):** Temperance (hard won), fortitude
**Cause opened:** 1947 | **Venerable declared:** 1975

A Dublin laborer from a poor family who became an alcoholic at thirteen, drinking heavily for sixteen years. At twenty-eight, he made a pledge of sobriety — initially for three months — on the advice of a priest. He never drank again. He spent the remaining forty-one years of his life in intense prayer, fasting, service to the poor, and manual labor. He died on his way to Mass in 1925. Workers who prepared his body for burial discovered that he had been wearing chains under his clothing as a form of penance. His cause for canonization is open.

**Key quotes:**
> "Never be too hard on the man who can't give up drink. It's as hard to give up the drink as it is to raise the dead to life again. But both are possible and even easy for Our Lord." *(attributed — widely cited, reported by those who knew him)*
> "I am not worthy that God should look at me." *(attributed — from his conversations with his confessor)*
> "Do not say you cannot overcome drink. Say you will not." *(attributed — his response to men who told him change was impossible)*

**The point:** His sobriety began with a three-month pledge — not a declaration of total change, not a dramatic transformation. One decision, for one limited period, with help. The argument he makes is that the beginning of recovery does not require certainty about the end; it requires the next small act. He is directly relevant for conversations about addiction, dependency, and the difference between "I cannot" and "I will not."

**In conversation:** *"Matt Talbot drank heavily from the time he was thirteen. At twenty-eight he walked into a church, made a pledge to stay sober for three months, and kept it. Then three more months. For forty-one years. He later said: 'Never be too hard on the man who can't give up drink — it's as hard as raising the dead. But both are possible for God.' He knew from the inside how hard it was. He also knew it was possible. Those two things together are the whole argument."*

**Age group:** Young adults (18+). Also useful for parents of young people struggling with substance use. His initial pledge model is practically useful: the argument that change begins with a bounded, specific commitment rather than a promise of total transformation.
