# Saints Perpetua and Felicity (died approx. 203 AD)

**Feast day:** March 7 | **Beatitude(s):** Persecuted for righteousness | **Virtue(s):** Fortitude, faith

Two North African women martyred in Carthage under Septimius Severus. Perpetua was a young noblewoman, educated, nursing a newborn infant, recently baptized. Felicity was her pregnant slave. Perpetua's own diary of her imprisonment is one of the earliest surviving documents written by a Christian woman. She recorded her father's repeated visits to her in prison — his grief and his pleas to recant — and her own grief at leaving her son. Felicity gave birth in prison two days before their execution.

**Key quotes:**
> "Father, do you see this vessel lying here? Can it be called by any other name than what it is?" He said no. "Nor can I call myself anything other than what I am — a Christian." *(documented — Perpetua's diary, her conversation with her father in prison)*
> "I cannot call myself anything other than what I am, a Christian." *(documented — her diary, summary form)*
> On her son being taken away to be raised by her family: "God arranged it so that the child no longer needed my nursing, and I suffered no inflammation; and I was not tortured with grief for him." *(documented — her diary)*

**The point:** Perpetua's diary is a first-person account, not hagiography written at a remove. She recorded the grief — her father's grief, her own grief over her son — without flinching. She was not indifferent to what she was losing; she was clear about what she was holding. Her dialogue with her father is a model of respectful but firm refusal to deny her identity under family pressure.

**In conversation:** *"Perpetua wrote a diary in prison while she was nursing her newborn. Her father came to her repeatedly and begged her to recant — to say she wasn't a Christian, just say it, save her life. She told him: 'You can see this vessel on the floor. Can you call it anything other than what it is? Neither can I call myself anything other than what I am.' She was executed in the arena. The diary survived. We have her actual words."*

**Age group:** Young adults (18+). Perpetua's diary is particularly useful because it is a primary source — first-person account of a young woman reasoning through a mortal decision.

**Primary source:** *The Passion of Perpetua and Felicity* — widely available online
