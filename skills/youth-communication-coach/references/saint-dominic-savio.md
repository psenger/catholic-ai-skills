# Saint Dominic Savio (1842–1857)

**Feast day:** March 9 | **Beatitude(s):** Pure in heart, peacemakers | **Virtue(s):** Fortitude, purity
**Canonized:** 1954 | **Age at death:** 14

A pupil of Don Bosco at the Oratory of Turin, known for cheerfulness, a clear moral conscience, and a talent for defusing conflict. When two classmates faced off in a fight, each holding rocks, Savio walked between them and held up a crucifix. He was not physically imposing; he was fourteen. He died of pleurisy, and Don Bosco later wrote his life as a model of how ordinary young people can be formed in holiness through joy rather than severity.

**Key quotes:**
> "Death rather than sin." *(documented — his chosen personal motto, recorded by Don Bosco)*
> "I am very happy, for I have nothing to trouble my conscience." *(documented — his reported words on his deathbed)*
> "Holiness consists in always being joyful." *(attributed, in the spirit of Don Bosco's teaching about him)*

**The point:** Moral courage in peer environments is possible at a young age and does not require being the strongest or most popular person in the room. Savio's interventions were not dramatic speeches — they were immediate, physical acts. He walked between the rocks. The argument he makes is that a formed conscience produces concrete action, not just good intentions.

**In conversation:** *"Don Bosco wrote about a student of his named Dominic Savio — fourteen years old, not especially tough, not the biggest kid. When a fight was about to break out, he walked between the two of them and held up a cross. Not a lecture. Just a physical act that changed the situation. What do you think gave him the nerve to do that?"*

**Age group:** Children (9–11), early teenagers (12–14). Don Bosco's relationship with him is also a model for adults: he accompanied, he observed, he did not micromanage.
