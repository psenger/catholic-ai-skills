# Saint Paul the Apostle (died approx. 64–68 AD)

**Feast day:** June 29 (with Peter) | **Beatitude(s):** Persecuted for righteousness (both as persecutor and as the persecuted) | **Virtue(s):** Faith, fortitude, charity

Before his conversion, Paul — then Saul of Tarsus — actively facilitated the death of Christians, including holding the coats of the men who stoned Stephen. He described himself after conversion as the *"foremost of sinners"* — using a present tense, not a past tense. He did not minimize or explain away what he had done. After the road to Damascus, he became the primary missionary of the early Church, was imprisoned, shipwrecked, beaten, and eventually martyred.

**Key quotes:**
> "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners — of whom I am the foremost." *(documented — 1 Timothy 1:15, Scripture — note the present tense)*
> "For me, to live is Christ, and to die is gain." *(documented — Philippians 1:21, Scripture)*
> "I can do all things through him who strengthens me." *(documented — Philippians 4:13, Scripture)*
> "Nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord." *(documented — Romans 8:38–39, Scripture)*

**The point:** He did not use his conversion to retroactively minimize what he had done. He called himself the foremost of sinners — present tense — after becoming a Christian. This is theologically precise: the grace that saved him did not erase the weight of his past, but it did not let the weight of his past determine his future. For any young person who believes their past actions have permanently defined them, Paul's self-description is the counter-argument from within the tradition.

**In conversation:** *"Paul helped kill Christians before he became one. He didn't hide it afterward — he called himself 'the foremost of sinners' in his own letters. Not was. Is. He understood that mercy doesn't delete your past; it changes what your past has power to do. He became the missionary who planted the Church across the Roman Empire. His starting point was the worst thing he could have done."*

**Age group:** Young adults (18+). Also useful for teenagers who feel permanently defined by something they have done.
