# Saint Thomas More (1478–1535)

**Feast day:** June 22 | **Beatitude(s):** Persecuted for righteousness | **Virtue(s):** Justice, fortitude, prudence
**Canonized:** 1935

Lord Chancellor of England under Henry VIII — a lawyer, scholar, husband, and father of four. When Henry claimed supremacy over the Church of England and required an oath affirming it, More refused — not publicly attacking the king, but refusing to affirm what he believed to be false. He was imprisoned for over a year in the Tower, tried on perjured testimony, and beheaded. His letters from prison to his daughter Margaret are documented and show him working through exactly what he was choosing and what it would cost his family. He was not without fear; he was afraid and he held his position anyway.

**Key quotes:**
> "I die the King's good servant, but God's first." *(documented — his last words on the scaffold)*
> "Nothing can come but that God wills. And I make me very sure that whatsoever that be, seem it never so bad in sight, it shall indeed be the best." *(documented — from a letter to his daughter Margaret from the Tower)*
> "I will not meddle with the consciences of others... but I will keep mine own." *(documented — consistent with his position throughout his trial)*
> To Margaret, who urged him to take the oath: "Is it not strange to you, my dear daughter, that your poor father should be one of the few in this so great a realm who will not agree to this matter?" *(documented — from his prison letters)*

**The point:** He was afraid. His prison letters to Margaret document that. He was not performing courage; he was making a genuine and costly choice against the pressure of the most powerful man in England. The argument he makes is that conscience is not a feeling but a claim — it can be wrong, it must be formed, but it cannot be surrendered to external pressure without destroying the person. His distinction between "the King's good servant" and "God's first" is not defiance; it is a statement of priority.

**In conversation:** *"Thomas More was Lord Chancellor of England — the most powerful legal position in the country under the king. Henry VIII asked him to sign a document More believed was a lie. More refused. He didn't argue publicly, didn't write pamphlets against the king. He just wouldn't sign. He spent over a year in the Tower. He was afraid — his letters to his daughter say so plainly. On the scaffold, his last words were: 'I am the King's good servant, but God's first.' That's the whole argument in eleven words."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (15–17), young adults (18+). The strongest available figure for conversations about conscience, integrity, and the cost of holding a position when power demands otherwise.

**Patron:** lawyers, politicians, statesmen
