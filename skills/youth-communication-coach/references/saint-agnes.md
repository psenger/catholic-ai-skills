# Saint Agnes (approx. 291–304)

**Feast day:** January 21 | **Beatitude(s):** Pure in heart, persecuted for righteousness | **Virtue(s):** Fortitude, purity
**Age at death:** approximately 12–13

A Roman girl who refused marriage to the son of a Roman prefect, having consecrated her virginity to Christ. She was subjected to various coercions before her execution. She said of her faith: it was not something external imposed on her but something she had already freely chosen. Her name became intertwined in Latin tradition with *agnus* (lamb) — she is depicted in art with a lamb, the symbol both of innocence and of Christ.

**Key quotes:**
> "I have already chosen a spouse who cannot be seen with bodily eyes. His beauty surpasses the sun and the moon." *(traditional, documented in early hagiographies)*
> "Christ made my soul beautiful with the jewels of grace and virtue." *(traditional attribution, Ambrose of Milan's account)*
> "I can lose my life but not my honor." *(traditional)*

**The point:** Bodily integrity is not negotiable, and the pressure to surrender it does not become more legitimate when it comes from someone with social power. Agnes was twelve. She refused a prefect's son. The argument she makes is that faith provides an identity stable enough to withstand social coercion — and that this is not rigidity but freedom.

**In conversation:** *"Agnes was twelve years old when a Roman official tried to force her into marriage. She turned him down. Not because she was naive about what that meant, but because she already knew who she was. She said she had already chosen a spouse. That kind of clarity, at twelve, is what the Church is pointing to when it talks about identity in Christ."*

**Age group:** Children (older, 10–11), early teenagers (12–15).
