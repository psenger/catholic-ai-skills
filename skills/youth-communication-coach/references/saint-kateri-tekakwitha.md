# Saint Kateri Tekakwitha (1656–1680)

**Feast day:** July 14 | **Beatitude(s):** Persecuted for righteousness, pure in heart | **Virtue(s):** Fortitude, faith
**Canonized:** 2012

A Mohawk woman who lost her parents and a brother to a smallpox epidemic at age four and was left with impaired vision and facial scarring. She converted to Christianity after contact with Jesuit missionaries, suffered persistent persecution from her tribe, and walked two hundred miles in winter to reach a Christian mission community near Montreal. She died at twenty-four. She is the first Native American saint.

**Key quotes:**
> "Who can tell me what is most pleasing to God, that I may do it?" *(documented — her reported question at her first serious encounter with Christianity, recorded by Jesuit missionaries)*
> "I am not my own. I have given myself to Jesus." *(attributed)*
> "I leave you and do not know whether I shall ever see you again. But let us remember each other always, and find strength in prayer." *(attributed — to a friend on her departure)*

**The point:** She chose her faith over her community of origin — over everything familiar, at significant personal cost, in winter, on foot. She is the strongest available argument for a young person who feels that living their faith means losing their identity or social belonging. The answer she embodies is that belonging to Christ is itself an identity — not an addition to who you are but the thing that makes you most fully who you are.

**In conversation:** *"Kateri Tekakwitha was Mohawk. After she converted, her tribe made her life very difficult. They mocked her, interfered with her practice, made her work on Sundays. She eventually walked two hundred miles in winter to reach a community where she could live her faith openly. She was twenty-four when she died. The Church canonized her in 2012. She gave up her entire world to be who she actually was."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (14–17), young adults (18+). Particularly useful for young people in communities where faith is countercultural or where choosing their faith means social friction.

**Patron:** environment, ecology, exiles, people ridiculed for their faith
