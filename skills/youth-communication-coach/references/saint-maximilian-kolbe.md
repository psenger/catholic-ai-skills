# Saint Maximilian Kolbe (1894–1941)

**Feast day:** August 14 | **Beatitude(s):** Merciful, peacemaker | **Virtue(s):** Charity, fortitude
**Canonized:** 1982

A Polish Franciscan priest who founded a major Catholic media organization and a radio station before being arrested by the Gestapo. In Auschwitz, after ten men were selected for death by starvation as reprisal for an escape attempt, he stepped forward from the line and offered to take the place of one of the ten — Francis Gajowniczek, a married man with children. The commander, after a moment, allowed it. Kolbe survived weeks in the starvation bunker, praying and singing hymns with the other men. He was finally killed by lethal injection. Gajowniczek survived the war and lived to ninety-three, attending Kolbe's canonization.

**Key quotes:**
> "The most deadly poison of our time is indifference." *(attributed — widely cited)*
> "No one in the world can change Truth. What we can do and should do is to seek truth and to serve it when we have found it." *(attributed)*
> "I am a Catholic priest." *(documented — his words when he stepped out of line at Auschwitz)*

**The point:** He stepped out of line and gave his name. That is the entirety of what he did. There was no sermon, no argument — just a man identifying himself and his reason. His act is the most direct available illustration of John 15:13: greater love has no one than to lay down their life for another. The argument is not abstract; it happened at Auschwitz in 1941 and Francis Gajowniczek was in the crowd at the canonization.

**In conversation:** *"In 1941, at Auschwitz, ten men were chosen to die in a starvation cell as punishment for an escape. One of them began weeping, saying his wife and children would never know what happened to him. A Polish priest stepped out of line and said: 'I will take his place. I am a Catholic priest.' The commander allowed it. The priest's name was Maximilian Kolbe. He led the other men in prayer and hymns until he was the last one alive. The man he saved, Francis Gajowniczek, lived to ninety-three and was in the crowd when Kolbe was canonized."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (14–17), young adults (18+).
