# Blessed Franz Jägerstätter (1907–1943)

**Feast day:** May 21 | **Beatitude(s):** Persecuted for righteousness | **Virtue(s):** Justice, fortitude
**Beatified:** 2007

An Austrian farmer, husband, and father of three daughters who refused induction into the German army after the Nazi annexation of Austria. He was not a pacifist; he distinguished between a just war, which he would have fought, and what he determined to be an unjust one. His bishop, his priest, and family friends all urged him to comply. He refused. He wrote extensive notes in prison explaining his reasoning. He was beheaded by guillotine in Berlin in 1943. His wife Franziska, who outlived him by decades and supported his beatification, said: "He did what he believed God called him to do. I did what I believed God called me to do — I supported him and raised our daughters."

**Key quotes:**
> "I cannot and may not take an oath in support of a government that is fighting an unjust war." *(documented — from his prison writings)*
> "I am firmly convinced that it is still best that I speak the truth, even if it costs me my life." *(documented — from his prison writings)*
> "I can say from my own experience how painful life often is when one lives as a halfway Christian. It is more like vegetating than living." *(documented — from his letters)*

**The point:** His resistance was theologically argued, not merely emotional. He studied, thought, prayed, and consulted. Every authority he respected told him he was wrong and he should comply. He held his position against that unanimous counsel — not because he was arrogant but because he had done the work and reached a conclusion he could not abandon. For young people being asked to rationalize what they know to be wrong by pointing to everyone else doing it, he is the clearest available example.

**In conversation:** *"In 1943 an Austrian farmer named Franz Jägerstätter was told to serve in the Nazi army. His bishop told him to go. His priest told him to go. His friends and family told him to go. He had a wife and three daughters. He had thought it through carefully and concluded that he could not. He was beheaded in August 1943. His wife later said: 'He did what he believed God called him to do.' The Church beatified him in 2007."*

**Age group:** Young adults (18+). Particularly powerful for conversations about following conscience against the majority, and about the difference between conformity and obedience.
