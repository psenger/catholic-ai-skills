# Saint Peter the Apostle (died approx. 64–68 AD)

**Feast day:** June 29 | **Beatitude(s):** Mourning (he wept bitterly), merciful (receiving mercy) | **Virtue(s):** Faith (imperfect and real), fortitude

Chosen by Jesus as the first among the Apostles, Peter declared "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God" (Mt 16:16) — and was told he was the rock on which the Church would be built. He also sank while walking on water (Mt 14:30), objected to the washing of his feet (Jn 13:8), fell asleep in Gethsemane, drew a sword at the arrest, and denied knowing Christ three times on the night of the Passion — publicly, with oaths (Mt 26:74). Afterward "he went out and wept bitterly." After the Resurrection, Jesus appeared specifically to restore him: three questions, corresponding to the three denials. "Do you love me?" Three times. Peter said yes. Jesus gave him the charge. He was martyred in Rome.

**Key quotes:**
> "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God." *(documented — Matthew 16:16, Scripture)*
> "Lord, you know everything; you know that I love you." *(documented — John 21:17, Scripture — his third response to Jesus's question)*
> He went out and wept bitterly. *(documented — Matthew 26:75, Scripture — not a quote but a fact worth noting)*

**The point:** The structure of Peter's story is the structure of the Christian life: declaration, failure, grief, restoration, mission. Jesus did not replace him after the denials. He appeared for him, as he appeared for Thomas, and he asked not "why did you fail?" but "do you love me?" The restoration is active — it is a three-part conversation, not an absolution pronounced from a distance. The argument is that failure does not end the relationship with God; it changes its shape.

**In conversation:** *"Peter denied knowing Jesus three times — publicly, with oaths, on the night it mattered most. Afterward he went out and wept bitterly. After the Resurrection, Jesus appeared to him specifically. Not to rebuke him. He asked him one question, three times: 'Do you love me?' Once for each denial. Peter said yes each time. Jesus gave him the charge to lead the Church. The failure is in the story. It's not edited out. It's the turning point."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (14–17), young adults (18+). Use after a significant moral failure — particularly when shame is making a young person withdraw.
