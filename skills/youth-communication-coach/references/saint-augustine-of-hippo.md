# Saint Augustine of Hippo (354–430)

**Feast day:** August 28 | **Beatitude(s):** Mourning, pure in heart (through long struggle) | **Virtue(s):** Faith, temperance
**Doctor of the Church**

One of the most influential theologians in Christian history and one of the most honest accounts of moral failure. He lived for years in a relationship outside marriage, fathered a son, embraced Manichaean heresy, and was haunted for years by his inability to live chastely — he knew what was right and could not do it. His mother Monica prayed for him for seventeen years. After his baptism by Ambrose in 387, he became Bishop of Hippo and wrote the *Confessions* — the first autobiography in Western literature and still one of the most penetrating accounts of the interior life.

**Key quotes:**
> "Our heart is restless until it rests in thee." *(documented — Confessions, Book I, opening lines)*
> "Lord, make me chaste — but not yet." *(documented — Confessions, Book VIII — his honest account of his own ambivalence)*
> "Late have I loved you, beauty so ancient and so new, late have I loved you." *(documented — Confessions, Book X — his expression of regret and arrival)*
> "Love God and do what you will." *(documented — Homilies on the First Epistle of John — meaning: when you truly love God, your will is rightly ordered)*
> "You have made us for yourself, O Lord." *(documented — Confessions, Book I)*

**The point:** He knew what was right, wanted to want it, and could not yet choose it. That experience is not failure of intellect but failure of will — and he named it with extraordinary honesty, without excusing it. His conversion came not through a decisive act of willpower but through a moment of grace in a garden. The argument he makes is that the desire for God precedes the ability to choose him, and that God does not abandon people who are not yet ready.

**In conversation:** *"Augustine wrote one of the most honest lines in all of Christian literature: 'Lord, make me chaste — but not yet.' He was talking about himself. He knew what was right. He wanted to want it. He just couldn't choose it yet. He was in his thirties when that changed — not through willpower, but through something he couldn't fully explain. He went on to become a bishop and one of the greatest theologians the Church has ever produced. His starting point was 'not yet.'"*

**Age group:** Young adults (18+), primarily for the adult doing the coaching. Also useful when speaking with a young adult who knows what they should do and is not doing it — the argument is that this gap is recognized and named, not evidence of irredeemable failure.

**Works:** *Confessions* — widely available; Books I, VIII, and IX are the most relevant
