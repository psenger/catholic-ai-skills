# Saint Maria Goretti (1890–1902)

**Feast day:** July 6 | **Beatitude(s):** Pure in heart, merciful | **Virtue(s):** Fortitude, charity
**Canonized:** 1950 | **Age at death:** 11

A peasant girl near Rome who was stabbed twelve times by a neighbor attempting assault. Before she lost consciousness, she was asked if she forgave her attacker and said that she did. She died the following day. Her attacker, Alessandro Serenelli, was sentenced to thirty years. In prison, after nearly eight years, he reported a dream in which Maria appeared and offered him white lilies — each of which turned into flame in his hands. He converted, was released early, and spent the remainder of his life in a Franciscan monastery. He was present at her canonization in 1950.

**Key quotes:**
> "I forgive him with all my heart, and I want him to be with me in paradise." *(documented — her reported words before death, recorded by the attending physician)*
> "Alessandro is our brother." *(Assunta Goretti, Maria's mother, to the crowd at the canonization — documented)*
> "I am certain that Maria Goretti is in paradise and that she interceded for my conversion." *(Alessandro Serenelli, documented — letter published after his conversion)*

**The point:** The full story is not only Maria's courage — it is Alessandro's conversion. The mercy she extended from her deathbed was not a passive or sentimental act; it set in motion a change that took eight years to complete. This saint is about the long reach of forgiveness, not only the moment of refusing coercion. Use the complete story, not only the martyrdom.

**In conversation:** *"Maria Goretti's story is usually told as a story about purity. But the other half — the half people skip — is what happened to the man who killed her. He spent eight years in prison with no sign of change, and then he had a dream. By the time she was canonized, he was in the crowd. Her mother looked out at him and said: 'Alessandro is our brother.' That's the whole story."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (13–17), young adults (18+) — with care and context. Do not reduce her to a symbol; use the complete narrative.
