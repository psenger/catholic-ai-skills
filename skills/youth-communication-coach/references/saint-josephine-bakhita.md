# Saint Josephine Bakhita (1869–1947)

**Feast day:** February 8 | **Beatitude(s):** Merciful, pure in heart | **Virtue(s):** Charity, fortitude
**Canonized:** 2000

A Sudanese woman kidnapped into slavery at approximately seven years old. She was given the name Bakhita — meaning "lucky" — by her captors because she had forgotten her own name from trauma. She was sold multiple times, tattooed by force, subjected to sustained violence. Through Italian masters she encountered the Canossian Sisters in Venice, converted to Christianity, and entered religious life. She became known throughout northern Italy for her gentleness and warmth.

**Key quotes:**
> "If I were to meet those who kidnapped me and even those who tortured me, I would kneel and kiss their hands — for if that did not happen, I would not be a Christian and a Religious today." *(documented — her reported words, widely recorded by the Canossian Sisters)*
> "Be good, love the Lord, pray for those who do not know Him. What a great grace it is to know God." *(documented — from her conversations in the convent)*
> "I am definitively loved and whatever happens to me — I am awaited by this Love." *(attributed — a summary of her spiritual position)*

**The point:** She forgave with specificity, not sentimentality. She named what happened to her — she did not minimize it — and she named where it led. Her gratitude is not denial; it is the genuine theological position that God can bring good from evil without that evil becoming acceptable. She is the strongest available figure for conversations about trauma, forgiveness of serious harm, and what faith looks like after genuine suffering.

**In conversation:** *"Josephine Bakhita was enslaved at seven years old. She was sold multiple times, tattooed against her will, treated brutally. She converted to Christianity as an adult and became a nun. She said later: if she met the people who kidnapped and tortured her, she would kneel and kiss their hands — because without that, she would never have found God. She's not saying what happened to her was fine. She's saying God was present in it anyway. That's different."*

**Age group:** Young adults (18+). Requires care and context. Particularly relevant for conversations about forgiveness of serious harm — abuse, assault, significant trauma. Do not use her story to suggest a young person should rush to forgive; use it to show that forgiveness of serious harm is possible and specific, not merely rhetorical.

**Patron:** Sudan, trafficking survivors, enslaved people
