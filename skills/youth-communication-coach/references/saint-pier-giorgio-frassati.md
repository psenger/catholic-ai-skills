# Blessed Pier Giorgio Frassati (1901–1925)

**Feast day:** July 4 | **Beatitude(s):** Poor in spirit, merciful, pure in heart | **Virtue(s):** Charity, justice
**Beatified:** 1990 | **Age at death:** 24

A Turin university student from a wealthy newspaper-owning family who spent his time serving the poor, mountaineering, going to parties, joking with friends, arguing about politics, and receiving daily Communion. He carried the poor men he visited through the streets of Turin when they were too weak to walk. He contracted poliomyelitis while visiting the sick and died within a week. His family discovered only at his funeral how many poor people knew him — people the family had never heard of, who filled the streets. His personal motto, carved on his ice axe: *Verso l'alto* — Toward the heights.

**Key quotes:**
> "Verso l'alto." (Toward the heights) *(documented — his personal motto)*
> "I want to live and not merely vegetate." *(attributed — from his letters)*
> "I will be going up." *(documented — his reported reply when a friend asked if he was getting better, spoken as he was dying)*
> "Charity is not enough; we need social reform." *(attributed — reflects his political and social convictions)*
> "Jesus comes to me every morning in Holy Communion. I return the visit by going to the poor." *(attributed — widely cited)*

**The point:** He integrated the Eucharist and service in the most literal way: he received Communion in the morning and then went to the poor in the afternoon. He did not experience his faith as separate from his social life, his physical life, or his political convictions. He is the strongest available argument against the compartmentalization of faith, especially for young adults who assume holiness requires leaving ordinary life behind.

**In conversation:** *"Pier Giorgio Frassati climbed mountains, went to parties, was studying engineering, argued politics, served the poor in Turin, and went to Mass every morning. He was twenty-four when he died. At his funeral, people came from all over the city — poor people his family had never met, people he had been visiting and helping for years, quietly, without telling anyone. His personal motto was carved on his ice axe: 'Toward the heights.' He meant both."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (16–17), young adults (18–25). The most versatile young adult saint. Use him for conversations about faith and ordinary life, social justice, Eucharist, vocation, and the integration of what you believe with how you actually live.
