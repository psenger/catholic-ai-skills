# Saint Thomas the Apostle (1st century)

**Feast day:** July 3 | **Beatitude(s):** Mourning (receiving comfort), meek | **Virtue(s):** Faith (born through encounter, not preceding it)

One of the Twelve, who refused to believe the Resurrection on the testimony of the other disciples: *"Unless I see the mark of the nails in his hands and put my finger into the mark of the nails and my hand into his side, I will not believe."* (John 20:25) Jesus appeared again specifically for him, a week later, and invited him to examine the wounds. Thomas responded: *"My Lord and my God!"* Jesus said: *"Have you believed because you have seen me? Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have come to believe."* (John 20:29)

**Key quotes:**
> "Unless I see the mark of the nails in his hands... I will not believe." *(documented — John 20:25, Scripture)*
> "My Lord and my God!" *(documented — John 20:28, Scripture — the most direct statement of Jesus's divinity in the Gospels, and it comes from the doubter)*

**The point:** Jesus did not rebuke Thomas for his doubt, exclude him from the community, or demand he perform belief he did not feel. He appeared for him specifically. Thomas's requirement for evidence was met. The encounter produced the clearest verbal statement of Christ's divinity in the New Testament — not from a confident believer, but from the one who doubted. For any young person who fears their questions disqualify them: the doubter is the one who said it plainest.

**In conversation:** *"Thomas wouldn't believe the Resurrection on anyone else's word. He said: I need to see it myself. Jesus showed up again, specifically for Thomas — a week later — and invited him to examine the wounds. Thomas said: 'My Lord and my God.' That's the clearest statement of who Jesus is in all four Gospels. It came from the one who doubted. Jesus didn't tell him he should have believed sooner. He appeared for him."*

**Age group:** Teenagers (14–17), young adults (18+). Use directly when a young person says their doubts mean they are not really Catholic, or that faith requires suppressing questions.
